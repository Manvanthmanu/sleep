Sleep is a natural, easily reversible, periodic state of many living things that are marked by the absence of wakefulness. While sleep has often been studied in mammals using neural patterns, behavioral criteria are typically used for a wide range of organisms. These behavioral criteria include quiescence that is reversible upon stimulation, decreased sensory responsiveness, and rebound sleep following sleep deprivation. Every closely examined animal including invertebrates such as mollusks, cuttlefish, octopuses, several species of insects, and even a jellyfish, which lacks a centralized brain has shown to exhibit sleep based on these criteria. To better understand sleep and its characteristics, an invertebrate Drosophila melanogaster, commonly known as the fruit fly was used as a model organism due to its high genetic resemblance with human beings. Sleep in Drosophila is associated with prolonged physical immobility, hence, measuring their locomotor behavior is the most practical technique to record sleep. In our experiment, a signal is collected when a single fly breaks an infrared light beam transecting the midline of a small recording chamber, which is a standard method for measuring circadian rhythms. Measurement of infrared beam split. Individual flies are inserted in glass tubes and secured on one end with food and on the other end with cotton. A beam of infrared light passes through the tube's midpoint. A computer is connected to a collector, which keeps track of how many times the fly breaches the infrared beam, this gives us the raw data. It helps determine whether there is too much or too little moment. The number acquired for each minute in the raw data corresponds to how many times the fly crosses the beam. The value '0' denotes inactivity.
